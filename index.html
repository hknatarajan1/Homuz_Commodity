Oil Price Model — First Principles

Gross disruption: ~9.7 mb/d (Iraq −3.0, Kuwait −2.6, Qatar −0.6, Bahrain −0.2, Saudi −2.5, UAE −0.8). Realistic offsets: ~4.3 mb/d — Yanbu net ~1.5 mb/d (port cap at 4.5 mb/d less ~2 mb/d Saudi refinery consumption), Fujairah ~1.0 mb/d (terminal hit Mar 16 and at capacity), Russia ~1.5 mb/d (US 30-day waiver, shadow fleet saturated), non-OPEC ramp ~0.3 mb/d. Net structural deficit: ~5.4 mb/d = 5.1% of global demand (105 mb/d). Short-run price elasticity −0.05 (academic consensus for acute supply shocks under 3 months; IEA range −0.02 to −0.15, Goldman-implied ~−0.05 for sustained 120-day closure): gross ceiling 5.1% ÷ 0.05 = ~$148/bbl before IEA buffer, bypass and supply offsets. At base assumptions, effective deficit narrows to ~3.0 mb/d by W7 (after 2.0 mb/d selective passage, 2.6 mb/d bypass, 2.4 mb/d non-Gulf offset), yielding W7 model peak ~$139/bbl base case (fundamental +57%, panic +34pp at $25/bbl). Higher analyst estimates ($147–$153) reflect pessimistic offset assumptions. IEA 400 Mb release suppresses prices W1–4; exhausted W5. Recession demand destruction minimal within 12 weeks; modelled at −0.5 mb/d from W9.

Insurance & Shipping — A Structural Price Driver

The strait was closed commercially by the insurance market, not just military force. P&I (Protection & Indemnity) cover was withdrawn from March 5. War risk premiums jumped from 0.125% to 0.4% of hull value per transit — for a $100M VLCC, a $275,000 increase per voyage, passed directly into freight rates and landed costs (+$3–5/bbl on crude). Critically, insurance does not return with a ceasefire — underwriters require sustained stability before reissuing cover, creating a 2–4 week commercial lag on top of any political resolution. Ships diverting to the Cape of Good Hope add 10–14 days and $1–2M per round voyage in additional bunker fuel. At diesel prices of $240+/bbl, this premium compounds further. The net effect: every commodity that moves by sea — which is all of them — carries an embedded Cape rerouting cost of $3–8 per tonne depending on route and vessel type. This is baked into all price projections in this model and sustains the premium even as the fundamental supply deficit is the primary driver.

Storage Cascade & Refinery Shock

Gulf storage was ~100 Mb remaining (Kayrros/JPMorgan Mar 2026). At 10 mb/d shut-in, exhausted in ~10 days — already past this in W3. Iraq, Kuwait, Qatar and Bahrain have zero bypass infrastructure and must simply stop producing. Production shut-ins require weeks to months to restart even after a ceasefire. Qatar LNG storage fills in 4 days at full rate — no buffer. CEO: no restart until conflict fully ends + 4–6 weeks technical restart. Refinery shock is separate from crude: ~1.8 mb/d of Gulf refining offline (Ras Tanura 550k b/d; Ruwais 817k b/d; Bapco 267k b/d). Gulf pre-war refined product exports 3.3 mb/d — now near zero. No spare global refining capacity. NW Europe jet fuel hit $218/bbl in W2 at $100 crude; crack spreads blow out further at $155+ crude, driving diesel and jet fuel above crude-only percentage gains.

Commodity Assumptions, Trajectories & Ceasefire Recovery

Each commodity has a distinct mechanism, a distinct peak timing, and a distinct post-ceasefire recovery profile. The table below documents the driver logic, key assumptions, slider sensitivity, and what happens when the Strait reopening slider is engaged. Commodities fall into four structural groups: energy-linked (track oil with amplification/dampening); supply-constrained (physical production offline regardless of price); input-cost lagged (60-day fertiliser/diesel pass-through); and biofuel-linked (energy-agri convergence via mandate policy).

Model caveats — two structural gaps:
(1) LNG tanker freight not captured. With Qatar offline and the Strait mined, ~60–70 LNG carriers normally operating Gulf routes are displaced. Rerouting Atlantic LNG to Asia (US to Japan via Cape) adds ~10–15 days voyage time versus normal routing, effectively removing ~15–20% of global LNG shipping capacity from the market. Freight rates spike independently of the commodity price. Real delivered JKM in Tokyo Bay could be $2–4/MMBtu higher than the benchmark price this model tracks — the commodity price is the floor, not the all-in delivered cost.
(2) Spot market liquidity — read as trajectory, not point forecast. Only ~30–35% of global LNG trades spot or short-term; the rest is locked under long-term contracts. In a genuine supply crisis, spot prices are set by the most exposed, least hedged buyers — often developing country importers who will pay almost any price for physical availability. The 2022 crisis saw JKM move $15–20/MMBtu in single sessions and briefly reach $70+. This model's smooth weekly progression is a central tendency. Actual realised weekly prices could range $35–55 with the average landing near $44 — the direction and plateau level are robust; the week-by-week path is not.
Coal ceiling — what it is and how it works:
The $45–50/MMBtu ceiling is a physical capacity constraint, not an economic switching threshold. A critical distinction: coal is already 4–6× cheaper than JKM on a pure energy basis at current prices. Newcastle 6,000 kcal/kg coal traded at $129.62/t in the week of Mar 6 (Argus/Reuters confirmed) — that is $5.45/MMBtu. At $32+ JKM, gas-to-coal switching is economically trivial. What limits it is physical dispatch capacity: how many coal megawatts can actually increase output.
The ceiling operates in three phases. Phase 1 (W3–W5): plants already holding coal inventory that were running at partial utilisation ramp to full output immediately — no new supply needed, ~15–20 GW incremental dispatch available. Korea's coal demand fell 17% in H1 2025 (IEA) and Japan has 33 GW of nuclear newly online (EIA Feb 2026), meaning both countries have meaningful coal headroom. Phase 2 (W5–W8): new coal procurement orders are placed; Newcastle coal rose +11.6% in W1–2 of the crisis (Argus Mar 12) and further price rises incentivise more Australian/Indonesian supply. Delivery lag: 4–6 weeks from order to arrival. Phase 3 (W8+): port congestion, shipping competition, and coal supply elasticity form the true ceiling — this is where $45–50 applies.
Restart friction cuts both ways. The previous framing — that coal restart friction would make the model too high — was backwards. High friction (mothballed plants taking 4–8 weeks to restart, boiler inspections, grid sync approvals) means less coal switching materialises in Phase 1–2, so the ceiling is higher and softer, not lower. Easy restarts (low friction) mean more coal supply comes online faster, the ceiling is firmer at $42–45, and the model could be ~$3–5/MMBtu too high in W7–W9. Given Japan retired 1,200 MW in three years and Korea is phasing out 40 of 61 units by 2040, some plant-level friction is likely, supporting the $45–50 range rather than $42–45.

HOUTHI RED SEA DISRUPTION — Second Chokepoint Scenario
What it is and why it matters: The Houthi slider models a resumption of Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping — a second independent chokepoint disruption on top of Hormuz. Base case is inactive as of W3: the Houthis issued a statement of intent to resume attacks on Israel-linked vessels (Mar 4) but no confirmed resumption has occurred. The ceasefire between Israel and Hamas has held, and Houthi operational tempo has not escalated. However, if Iran's position hardens or the conflict spreads, Houthi activation is plausible from W4–W6.

The mechanism is different from Hormuz: Hormuz is a supply disruption (oil/gas cannot leave the Gulf). Red Sea is a routing disruption — goods can still move but must reroute via Cape of Good Hope, adding 10–14 days and $1–2M per round voyage at current fuel costs.
Historical calibration (2023–24 precedent): IMF/BIMCO data: 57–65% overall Suez throughput reduction during peak Houthi activity. Container shipping: 75–90% reduction (Maersk, Hapag-Lloyd, MSC all suspended Red Sea transits). Crude tankers: 32–45% lower (operators took war-risk premium rather than rerouting — more risk tolerance than container lines). Product tankers: 19% lower. LNG carriers: not primarily Red Sea users (Qatar LNG routes east to Asia; Atlantic LNG to Europe rarely uses Suez at meaningful scale).

Stickiness: BIMCO Jan 2026 data showed Suez still 60% below pre-crisis levels 100 days after the last confirmed Houthi attack — operators are extremely slow to return.
Commodity impacts when active: TTF +€5–7/MWh — primary impact. Atlantic LNG (US/Nigeria) is Europe's marginal substitute for Qatar; must reroute Cape when Red Sea is unsafe, adding ~$1.5/MMBtu. This is the biggest single Houthi transmission channel in the model.
DAP/PE/aluminium +1.5–2pp — Gulf exports to Europe/Americas reroute Cape; freight premium embedded.
JKM: minimal — Asia-Pacific LNG is priced on Pacific supply/demand dynamics; Atlantic routing irrelevant to JKM.
Brent: marginal — crude tanker operators mostly took war risk in 2023–24 rather than rerouting; oil price impact was limited and already partially embedded in insurance premiums.

Ceasefire linkage: Auto-zeroes on Hormuz ceasefire — Iran's ceasefire is assumed to end material support and operational justification for Houthi Red Sea campaign simultaneously. Even after stand-down, 4–6 weeks of confirmed calm required before shipping companies resume Red Sea transits.
What it is: A successful strike destroying Kharg Island's oil export infrastructure. Kharg handles 90–95% of Iranian crude exports (~1.5 mb/d currently flowing to China via selective passage). This is a tail risk scenario — explicitly not a current scenario as of W3. Modelled because it is the single most consequential unilateral escalation within range, and the market needs to understand the pricing implications before it happens.
Permanent effects (not ceasefire-reversible): (1) Iranian selective passage → 0 mb/d permanently — Kharg is the only functional export terminal; reconstruction is years, not weeks. (2) Infrastructure damage floor locked at ASMP.infra + khargPP from strike week onwards — degrading bypass capacity and capping post-reopening recovery. Damage persists through any ceasefire. (3) Risk premium spikes to $45/bbl on strike week (regime-existential signal), then decays normally — panic resolves, damage does not.
Timing sensitivity: W5–W6 strike causes maximum incremental price damage — IEA buffer exhausted, selective passage fully priced in by markets, non-Gulf supply not yet ramped, demand destruction not yet started. W1–W3 less incremental (passage not yet credited by market). W8+ limited ceiling as demand destruction absorbs shock. Oil Price Model — First Principles

Gross disruption: ~9.7 mb/d (Iraq −3.0, Kuwait −2.6, Qatar −0.6, Bahrain −0.2, Saudi −2.5, UAE −0.8). Realistic offsets: ~4.3 mb/d — Yanbu net ~1.5 mb/d (port cap at 4.5 mb/d less ~2 mb/d Saudi refinery consumption), Fujairah ~1.0 mb/d (terminal hit Mar 16 and at capacity), Russia ~1.5 mb/d (US 30-day waiver, shadow fleet saturated), non-OPEC ramp ~0.3 mb/d. Net structural deficit: ~5.4 mb/d = 5.1% of global demand (105 mb/d). Short-run price elasticity −0.05 (academic consensus for acute supply shocks under 3 months; IEA range −0.02 to −0.15, Goldman-implied ~−0.05 for sustained 120-day closure): gross ceiling 5.1% ÷ 0.05 = ~$148/bbl before IEA buffer, bypass and supply offsets. At base assumptions, effective deficit narrows to ~3.0 mb/d by W7 (after 2.0 mb/d selective passage, 2.6 mb/d bypass, 2.4 mb/d non-Gulf offset), yielding W7 model peak ~$139/bbl base case (fundamental +57%, panic +34pp at $25/bbl). Higher analyst estimates ($147–$153) reflect pessimistic offset assumptions. IEA 400 Mb release suppresses prices W1–4; exhausted W5. Recession demand destruction minimal within 12 weeks; modelled at −0.5 mb/d from W9.

Insurance & Shipping — A Structural Price Driver

The strait was closed commercially by the insurance market, not just military force. P&I (Protection & Indemnity) cover was withdrawn from March 5. War risk premiums jumped from 0.125% to 0.4% of hull value per transit — for a $100M VLCC, a $275,000 increase per voyage, passed directly into freight rates and landed costs (+$3–5/bbl on crude). Critically, insurance does not return with a ceasefire — underwriters require sustained stability before reissuing cover, creating a 2–4 week commercial lag on top of any political resolution. Ships diverting to the Cape of Good Hope add 10–14 days and $1–2M per round voyage in additional bunker fuel. At diesel prices of $240+/bbl, this premium compounds further. The net effect: every commodity that moves by sea — which is all of them — carries an embedded Cape rerouting cost of $3–8 per tonne depending on route and vessel type. This is baked into all price projections in this model and sustains the premium even as the fundamental supply deficit is the primary driver.

Storage Cascade & Refinery Shock

Gulf storage was ~100 Mb remaining (Kayrros/JPMorgan Mar 2026). At 10 mb/d shut-in, exhausted in ~10 days — already past this in W3. Iraq, Kuwait, Qatar and Bahrain have zero bypass infrastructure and must simply stop producing. Production shut-ins require weeks to months to restart even after a ceasefire. Qatar LNG storage fills in 4 days at full rate — no buffer. CEO: no restart until conflict fully ends + 4–6 weeks technical restart. Refinery shock is separate from crude: ~1.8 mb/d of Gulf refining offline (Ras Tanura 550k b/d; Ruwais 817k b/d; Bapco 267k b/d). Gulf pre-war refined product exports 3.3 mb/d — now near zero. No spare global refining capacity. NW Europe jet fuel hit $218/bbl in W2 at $100 crude; crack spreads blow out further at $155+ crude, driving diesel and jet fuel above crude-only percentage gains.

Cascade Methodology & Food Lag

All non-energy commodities are recascaded from the revised oil trajectory. At $155+ oil, global gas prices rise, raising production costs for alternative fertiliser producers who were supposed to moderate prices — the replacement supply is itself now expensive. Naphtha and ethane (PE feedstocks) track crude directly. Diesel costs at $240+/bbl feed into every tonne of food and goods transported globally. Food lag: 60-day input-cost-to-consumer-price lag (RSM US). Spring planting in W3 — reduced fertiliser application locks in harvest shortfall regardless of when the crisis ends. W8–W12 food prices reflect both elevated input costs AND lower harvest volumes simultaneously. Urea calibrated against 2022 Ukraine spike (~$900/t); current disruption is deeper. Goldman: US CPI peaks 4.5%. Oxford Economics: eurozone/UK/Japan contraction at $140 average oil — this model's peak weeks are above that threshold.
